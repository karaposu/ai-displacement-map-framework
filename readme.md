# The AI Displacement Map

**A structural framework for predicting how AI reshapes any job or business.**

---

## Why This Framework Exists

For a while, we've been trying to figure out how AI will change the world. Not out of curiosity alone, but because if you can predict what's coming, you can prepare for it.

Our early approach was wrong. We were thinking bottom-up: "Engineers can now do X, drivers can now do Y, designers can now do Z…" This gets overwhelming fast. Too many details, too many variables, too many moving parts. You end up with scattered observations instead of a coherent picture.

What we were missing was a logical foundation: a core principle that explains *how* AI actually changes things, and a framework built on that principle, so we can reason clearly about what's coming instead of guessing.

## The Approach: Top-Down Thinking

Instead of cataloging every specific change (bottom-up), we start from first principles. Understand the fundamental ways AI affects our world, then use those principles to make accurate predictions about any specific scenario.

This framework is a top-down tool. It gives you a repeatable way to reason about any job, any business, any industry, any timeline, without getting lost in the details.

## The Core Hypothesis

AI doesn't replace jobs all at once. It disrupts them through **two channels**:

1. **Information Access**: AI makes it trivially easy to find, retrieve, and receive information that previously required time, expertise, or human intermediaries. Information itself is still valuable; what's changed is that *access* to it is no longer scarce.
2. **Action Automation**: AI makes it possible to *act on* that information automatically, replacing tasks that used to require a skilled human doing the work.

When both channels are affected, entire job functions start to dissolve. But not everything dissolves equally, because there's a third layer AI can't touch yet:

3. **The Human Experience Layer**: People don't just want results. They want the *process*. They want to spend time with others, feel things, discover things, be entertained. You can see this everywhere: people still browse bookstores for hours when they could find the same book online in 10 seconds, still post questions on Reddit instead of asking AI for an instant answer. The information isn't the point; the human interaction and the experience are. This layer resists automation, but it's not permanent. It shifts across generations.

And there's a dimension that accelerates everything:

4. **The Environment Multiplier**: No job or business exists in isolation. When the clients, suppliers, partners, and systems *around* it also adopt AI, compound effects emerge that are far larger than automating one role or operation alone. This effect is slow at first and explosive later.

## The Framework: 6 Steps to Analyze Any Job or Business

### Step 1: Map the Information Flow

For the job or operation in question, ask:

- **What information comes in?** (From clients, colleagues, systems, books, schedules, databases…)
- **What information goes out?** (Deliverables, reports, feedback, grades, designs…)
- **What information just gets routed?** Simply passing info from A to B: forwarding, scheduling, updating status…

### Step 2: Map the Actions

What actually gets *done* with the information?

- **Routine actions**: Repetitive, rule-based tasks (data entry, grading, scheduling, editing, formatting…)
- **Judgment actions**: Decisions that require understanding context (advising a client, choosing a creative direction, diagnosing a problem…)
- **Creative actions**: Producing something new (writing, designing, teaching a concept in a novel way…)
- **Physical actions**: Doing something in the real world (building, operating, being physically present…)

### Step 3: Estimate the Automation Speed

What actually determines automation speed is the **feedback loop**: how fast can you judge whether the AI's output is good or bad?

This matters because AI improves through iteration. Generate → evaluate → fix → generate again. The faster that loop runs, the faster AI gets good enough to automate the task. It's about how quickly you can assess whether the result is correct.

Score each action from Steps 1 and 2 on these four factors:

**1. Feedback loop speed (most important):** How fast can the output be evaluated?

| Speed | Example | Automation Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Seconds** | Code (run it, tests pass or fail), photo editing (look at it), math (check the answer) | Extremely fast automation. AI iterates hundreds of times in the time a human does it once. This is why code generation automated so aggressively despite being "creative" work |
| **Minutes to hours** | Writing a standard RFP (read it, is it usable?), categorizing spend data (spot-check a sample), drafting emails (read and approve) | Fast automation. Human reviews output quickly, AI learns what "good" looks like |
| **Days to weeks** | Supplier negotiation (did we get good terms?), marketing campaign (is it performing?), hiring (did we pick the right candidate?) | Slower automation. The loop takes too long for rapid AI improvement. Humans stay in the loop longer |
| **Months to years** | Teaching a student (did they actually learn?), strategic sourcing (was this the right supplier long-term?), investment decisions | Very slow automation. Output quality can't be judged quickly, so AI can't iterate and improve fast. These tasks resist automation even if they seem "simple" |

**2. How digital is the work?** Fully digital tasks automate faster because AI can operate directly on the material. Code is pure text. Procurement data lives in databases. These automate fast. Anything requiring a physical presence (construction, surgery, hands-on repair) waits for robotics, regardless of how fast the feedback loop is.

**3. How much training data exists?** AI needs examples to learn from. Code generation exploded because the internet is full of open-source code. Internal company processes (custom approval workflows, proprietary negotiation playbooks) are harder because the training data is scarce and private.

**4. How large is the economic incentive?** High-cost tasks get targeted first because the ROI on automation is bigger. This is why software engineering (high salaries, massive scale) saw aggressive AI investment before, say, school administration (lower salaries, fragmented market). Follow the money.

**How to use this**: For each action you mapped in Steps 1 and 2, score it on these four factors. Actions that are digital, have fast feedback loops, abundant training data, and high economic incentive will automate first, regardless of whether they're "routine" or "creative." Actions with slow feedback loops, physical requirements, scarce data, or low economic incentive will take much longer, even if they seem simple on the surface.

### Step 4: Identify the Human Experience Layer

Ask: **What part of this job exists because people want the experience, not just the result?**

- A birthday photoshoot isn't just about getting photos. It's about spending time together and having fun.
- Going to school isn't just about absorbing information. It's about socialization, structure, and growth.
- Going to a restaurant isn't just about food. It's about the atmosphere and company.

**These parts resist automation, but only for the current generation.**

### Step 5: Apply the Generational Drift

This is the critical time dimension most people miss. The Human Experience Layer is not fixed; it erodes as new generations grow up with AI as their normal.

**The principle**: Whatever you grew up with feels natural. Whatever arrived after you feels like a tool. A generation that grows up with AI-generated photos, AI tutors, and AI companions won't attach the same emotional value to a human photographer or a classroom teacher. They'll find *new* human experiences to value, ones we can't predict yet.

**How it works**:

- **Current generation** (grew up without strong AI): The human experience layer is thick. People still *want* a real photographer, a real teacher, a real waiter. Automation feels like a loss.
- **Next generation** (growing up *with* AI): The experience layer thins. AI tutoring feels normal. AI-generated images feel normal. They won't hire a photographer for a birthday; they'll do something else entirely for the human experience part. Families start teaching kids with AI at home because it feels natural, not radical.
- **Generation after that**: The experience layer has fully shifted. The jobs that survived on "people want the human touch" have either transformed into something unrecognizable or been replaced by entirely new human experiences we can't imagine today.

**This means**: When you run the analysis, don't just ask "does the human experience layer protect it?" Ask: **"For how long?"** A job or business protected by the experience layer today might have 5–10 years, not forever. The experience layer is a buffer, not a wall.

### Step 6: Factor in the Environment Multiplier

Steps 1–5 analyze the job or business in isolation. But nothing exists in a vacuum. Every role or operation sits inside an environment (clients, suppliers, partners, tools, other departments, regulators) and that environment is *also* getting AI-powered over time.

**The principle**: When the people and systems *around* a role or business also adopt AI, new capabilities emerge that wouldn't exist from automating it alone. The compound effect is much larger than any single automation. This is the Environment Multiplier.

**How to apply it**: After completing Steps 1–5 for the job itself, ask these questions:

- **Who does this role or business interact with?** List the key external parties: clients, vendors, partners, other departments, regulators, platforms.
- **How fast is *their* environment adopting AI?** If you're analyzing a procurement team, their suppliers are also getting AI agents. If it's a teacher, the parents and students are also getting AI tools. If it's a SaaS business, the customers' workflows are also being automated.
- **What becomes possible when both sides have AI?** This is where emergent capabilities appear. A procurement agent negotiating with a human supplier is fast. A procurement agent negotiating with a supplier's AI agent, where both sides can instantly model scenarios, counter-offer, and converge on terms, is a fundamentally different game. A teacher with AI tools is more effective. A teacher whose students all have personalized AI tutors at home changes *what the classroom is even for*.

**The key rule**: The focus stays on what you're analyzing. You're not trying to map every role in the ecosystem. You're asking: *"Given that the environment around this job or business is also improving with AI, how does that accelerate or change the displacement?"*

**How it affects the timeline**: The Environment Multiplier is slow at first and fast later. For the next six months or so, the environment around most jobs and businesses is still mostly human-operated, meaning the main driver of change is what happens *inside* a company, not between companies.

But by early 2027, AI adoption will be widespread enough that AI-to-AI interactions between businesses are no longer rare. One company's system negotiating, coordinating, or transacting directly with another's. By 2028, it becomes the expected way of doing business. That's the period when the compounding effects kick in hard. This is why even short-term predictions often look conservative in hindsight: they underestimate the ecosystem effect.

**Example patterns**:

- **Procurement buyer** → Suppliers get AI agents → agent-to-agent negotiation becomes the norm → the buyer's role shifts from "doing deals" to "designing the strategy their agents follow"
- **Photographer** → Clients get AI image generation tools → clients start producing their own content for low-stakes use cases → the photographer's remaining market shrinks to high-end, experience-driven work faster than expected
- **Teacher** → Students get personalized AI tutors at home → parents who see their kid learning faster with AI start questioning the value of classroom time → the teacher's role shifts from "delivering knowledge" to "facilitating social development" faster than the school system anticipated
- **Software engineer** → Product managers, designers, and QA testers all get AI tools → the entire product development cycle accelerates → the engineer's role shifts from "writing code" to "orchestrating AI agents across the whole development pipeline"

## How to Use This Framework

Pick any job or business. Run it through the six steps. You'll end up with a clear picture across time:

- **What disappears first**: Actions with fast feedback loops, digital workflows, abundant training data, and high economic incentive. These automate regardless of whether they seem "simple" or "complex." At this stage, the environment is mostly still human, so the impact comes mainly from internal AI adoption.
- **What transforms next**: Actions where AI can assist but the feedback loop is too slow for full autonomy. Humans stay in the loop to evaluate, override, and steer. The role or operation becomes 10x more effective with AI, but human oversight is still needed. The environment is starting to catch up, with clients, partners, and vendors adopting AI too, creating new compound effects.
- **What's protected for now** (current generation): The human experience layer. Wherever people value the process over the outcome, the job survives in some form.
- **What eventually shifts** (5–15 years): Even the experience layer. New generations redefine what "human experience" means. And by this point, the entire environment is AI-powered, so the compound effects of the Environment Multiplier are fully active. The job or business either evolves into something new or gets replaced by entirely different activities that fulfill the same human need.

## Example: Photographer

| Layer | Current State | ~1 Year | ~2 Years | ~3 Years |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Information Access** | Client calls, browses references manually | AI analyzes client's Instagram, auto-generates mood boards | Fully automated client profiling before first contact | Irrelevant. The role has transformed |
| **Routing** | Back-and-forth emails about scheduling, pricing, delivery | Fully automated | Fully automated | Fully automated |
| **Routine Actions** | Editing and color correction already ~95% AI-assisted. Remaining: album layout, file formatting, delivery, and print preparation | Most remaining routine steps automated | Full post-production pipeline is AI-driven end to end | No human involvement in any routine step |
| **Judgment/Creative** | Choosing angles, directing poses, reading the mood | AI suggests, human decides | AI generates full mock-ups; human curates and directs on-set | AI generates entire photorealistic scenes from prompts |
| **Human Experience** | The birthday shoot is a *memory*. People want to be there, laugh, feel something | **Still strong.** Current generation values the real experience | **Starting to thin.** Younger clients are comfortable with AI-generated content for many use cases | **Thinning fast.** The generation growing up with AI photos starts to see "hiring a photographer" differently. They find other ways to create memories |
| **Environment Multiplier** | Clients find photographers manually; venues, stylists, and print shops are all human-operated | Low impact. Clients use AI for mood boards but still interact with the photographer directly | **Accelerating.** Clients have their own AI image tools for everyday content. The photographer's low-end market (product shots, headshots, social content) evaporates because the *client's* AI can do it | **Compounding hard.** Social media platforms auto-generate styled photos. Venues have AI staging. The entire ecosystem around photography is automating, leaving only the high-end experience niche |

## Example: Teacher

| Layer | Current State | ~1 Year | ~2 Years | ~3 Years |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Information Access** | Reviews curriculum, reads textbooks, prepares lessons | AI auto-prepares lesson plans, adapts to class level | AI generates fully personalized curricula per student | AI knows each student better than any teacher could |
| **Routing** | Communicates grades, coordinates with staff, updates records | Fully automated | Fully automated | Fully automated |
| **Routine Actions** | Grading, creating quizzes, tracking progress | AI grades in real-time, identifies weak spots per student | AI runs continuous assessment. No more "test days" | All evaluation is continuous and invisible |
| **Judgment/Creative** | Explaining difficult concepts, adapting to struggling students, inspiring curiosity | AI assists with targeted questions and Socratic prompts | AI handles most 1-on-1 tutoring; teacher focuses on group dynamics and mentorship | AI tutors are genuinely better at personalized teaching than most humans |
| **Human Experience** | Kids need connection, mentorship, socialization. School is growing up, not just learning | **Still strong.** Parents and society value schools as social institutions | **Starting to shift.** Early-adopter families teach kids with AI at home. Homeschool + AI becomes a serious option | **Shifting faster than expected.** Parents who see their kid learning faster with AI start questioning classroom value. Schools feel the pressure |
| **Environment Multiplier** | Parents, textbook publishers, and school administrators are all human-operated. The student's learning environment is mostly offline | Low direct impact. Some students start using AI tutors at home, but the classroom experience is unchanged | **Accelerating.** Students arrive with AI tutors that already know their strengths and gaps. Parents get real-time AI dashboards on their kid's progress, and they start asking: "Why is the classroom slower than what my kid gets at home?" | **Compounding hard.** Every student has a personalized AI tutor. Textbook publishers replaced by AI curricula. The teacher's role is pressured from all sides because the *environment* is now smarter than the classroom |

## One More Thing: The Objectivity Advantage

AI doesn't have favorites. A teacher might give a higher grade to a student they like. An AI won't. As automation enters judgment-heavy tasks, **objectivity** becomes a side effect, sometimes welcome, sometimes uncomfortable, but always worth noting.

---

*This framework can be applied to any job, any business, any industry, any timeline. The question is always the same: where does information flow, what actions follow, how fast is the feedback loop, what part of it do humans want to keep doing themselves, for how long, and what happens when the entire environment around it gets AI-powered too?*
